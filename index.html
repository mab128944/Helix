What is Corn Silage? Strategic Advantages

Corn silage represents high-energy forage produced through the controlled anaerobic preservation of whole-plant maize. Unlike standard hay, it preserves essential starches, vitamins, and digestible fibers, acting as an optimal feed source for elite dairy herds.

Moisture Management Science

Harvesting corn within a precise 62% to 68% moisture range is essential. Deviating from these boundaries can disrupt lactic fermentation pathways, resulting in energy losses or improper runoff preservation.

Core Structural Advantages

Aerobic Stability via Compression

Consistent Daily Energy Rations

Consistent moisture and starch distributions help prevent sudden drops in milk volume across the herd.

Logistical Efficiency

Baled structures simplify handling, trackable storage, and transport across long distances without spoilage.

The Anaerobic Fermentation Timeline

Phase 1: Aerobic Phase (Hours 1–24)

Trapped oxygen is consumed by plant respiration, clearing the path for anaerobes.

Phase 2: Fermentation Phase (Days 2–21)

Lactic acid bacteria convert water-soluble carbohydrates, safely dropping the pH to a stable 3.8–4.2 range.

Phase 3: Stable Storage Horizon

As long as oxygen is sealed out, the silage retains its full nutritional profile indefinitely.
